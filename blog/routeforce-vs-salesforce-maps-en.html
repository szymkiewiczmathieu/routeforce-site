RouteForce vs Salesforce Maps: honest comparison 2026

Both tools run inside Salesforce, display accounts on a map, and calculate routes. The business model, target customer, and deployment constraints are very different. Here's what actually matters.

Salesforce Maps and RouteForce sit in the same category: native Salesforce mapping and route optimization tools. The question we hear often: "why not just use Salesforce Maps?"

Short answer: it depends on team size, budget, and what field reps actually need. Here are the concrete differences.

Pricing: where everything starts

Salesforce Maps starts at $75/user/month (Maps) and goes up to $125/user/month for Maps Advanced, billed annually. Source: official Salesforce pricing page, verified March 2026.

For a team of 15 reps on Maps Advanced, that's about $22,500/year just for the mapping license — on top of the Salesforce CRM licences already in place.

RouteForce works differently: €599/month excl. tax (billed annually), regardless of how many users are in the org. Self-setup is free; turnkey deployment with a RouteForce engineer is €3,000 excl. tax one-time. A 14-day free trial is available directly on routeforce.app — no sales call required.

Salesforce Maps prices are public on salesforce.com. RouteForce prices are those listed on this page at time of writing — check the flat-rate pricing page and the Salesforce route planning page for the current version.

Where Salesforce Maps is stronger

Straight talk: Salesforce Maps Advanced does more on territory analytics and long-horizon planning. If a team needs heat maps, geographic coverage analysis by sales territory, or multi-week capacity planning with complex scheduling logic, Maps Advanced is built for that.

Salesforce Maps also comes with enterprise support infrastructure: Success Plans, group contract integration, negotiable SLAs. For large organizations with strict contractual requirements, that's a real advantage.

Where RouteForce is different

RouteForce targets field teams that want to get moving fast. Deployment in hours, an interface designed for mobile (field reps work on phones, not laptops), and a pricing model that doesn't penalize team growth.

Click-to-call is a good example: a rep leaving a meeting and wanting to call the next contact on their route can do it directly from the map marker, without switching apps. That's a small thing that, repeated 10 times a day, adds up.

The French server (OVH Gravelines) addresses a concrete requirement for organizations with data localization policies — particularly in the public sector, insurance mutuals, or companies with contractual data sovereignty commitments.

Who should pick what

Salesforce Maps Advanced fits large organizations (50+ field reps) with territory analytics needs, a dedicated Salesforce admin, and a budget that can absorb per-user costs.

RouteForce fits teams of 5 to 30 reps who want an operational tool quickly, mobile-first, without a cost that grows with the team.

There's an overlap zone — mid-sized teams where both could work. In that case, the deciding factor usually comes down to whether territory analytics (Maps) or field simplicity with click-to-call and integrated visit reports (RouteForce) takes priority.
