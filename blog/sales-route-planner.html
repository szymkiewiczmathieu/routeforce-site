Sales route planner: what teams should compare before they buy

A sales route planner should not only reduce drive time. It should help the team plan better, execute better, and keep customer activity visible in the CRM.

Why teams start looking for a sales route planner

Sales reps lose time when route planning lives in spreadsheets, Google Maps tabs, and disconnected notes. A proper sales route planner should turn customer lists into a workable day without forcing reps into admin overhead.

What buyers should compare

When evaluating a sales route planner, teams should compare across several dimensions:

  • Route sequencing quality — how efficiently stops are ordered and re-ordered when plans change
  • Native vs. integrated — whether the tool runs inside Salesforce or syncs data from an external platform
  • Visit creation and execution workflow — including check-in/check-out tracking and visit reporting tied to CRM records
  • Territory management — territory drawing, assignment, and balanced workload distribution
  • Mobile experience — whether reps can plan, navigate, and log visits from the field
  • Pricing model as the team grows — per-user fees vs. flat org pricing, and what happens at 10, 20, or 50 users
  • Reporting visibility — whether managers see field activity, visit outcomes, and route adherence in Salesforce dashboards
  • Setup complexity — time to deploy and whether admins can configure the tool without outside consultants

How the most-cited options compare

Several tools appear regularly when teams research a sales route planner for Salesforce:

  • Badger Maps — mobile-first route planner with multi-CRM integrations, route optimization, check-ins, and territory drawing. Popular with individual reps, but runs outside Salesforce as a separate app.
  • Salesforce Maps — native Salesforce product at $75–$150/user/month. Full-featured, but costs scale quickly: a 20-user team can reach $1,500–$3,000/month before adding optimization add-ons.
  • Map My Customers — offers territory views, multi-stop route building, and simple activity planning. Integrates with Salesforce but is not native; data syncs between two systems.
  • Route4Me — logistics-oriented route planner that supports many stops per route. Designed more for delivery and service fleets than CRM-driven field sales workflows.
  • OptimoRoute — strong optimization engine for scheduling and routing. Better suited for dispatch-heavy teams than Salesforce-centric sales organizations.
  • portatour — automatic visit planning based on customer importance, visit frequency, and geographic distance. Integrates with Salesforce but manages its own scheduling logic outside the CRM.

Why workflow fit matters

A planner that optimizes routes but breaks the CRM workflow creates another kind of inefficiency. External tools require data sync, which introduces lag, duplicates, and a second system for reps to learn. That is why many teams prefer route planning that stays inside Salesforce rather than a disconnected planning layer.
